Practice Block — Simulations 1–4

Run Sims 1–3 before Sim 4. The adversarial scenario requires the Phase framework and drift system as anchors.

Sim 1 · Phase 1–4 Plan Construction
A school has accepted the recommendation to install the Matching Engine. The owner has committed to a 90-day rollout. Design the complete Phase 1–4 plan.
All four phases required. Phase 1 must name an owner and an audit mechanism. Phase 3 must name a drift threshold and a review cadence.
Phase 1 — Preparation (days 1–7)
Blueprint finalized. Sole owner: Front Desk Coordinator (ACA data collection). 30-day adoption target: 80% of new students with ACA profile by day 30. Audit mechanism: CRM weekly report — % of new students with completed ACA. Early adopter identified and briefed.
Phase 2 — Deployment (days 8–14)
30-minute role-based training on ACA collection and CRM entry. SOP printed and posted at front desk. Analyst available for first-week questions. All issues documented for Phase 3.
Phase 3 — Stabilization (days 15–60)
Weekly Rhythm review: ACA completion rate vs. 80% target. Drift threshold: any week where completion rate fails to improve 3+ pts across 3 consecutive reviews. Friction audit if below 60% at week 2.
Phase 4 — Optimization (days 61–90)
Month 2 MSOI recalculation vs. pre-implementation baseline. Match score distribution reviewed. Next priority failure identified. ACA completion data fed into GC Intelligence Layer.
Sim 2 · Adoption Benchmark Application · No reference cards
Three systems. Three current adoption rates. For each: cite the 30-day benchmark, determine pass/fail, and name the drift lever if drift has occurred.
Scenario A: Lesson note submission at 61% — week 4
Benchmark: ≥90% by day 30. Status: FAILED — 29 points below target. Drift lever: Friction (below 70% at week 2 threshold). Recovery: friction audit immediately — form likely too long or submission path too complex.
Scenario B: Trial follow-up at 88% — week 1
Benchmark: 85%+ by end of week 1. Status: PASS — 3 points above target. No drift action required. Continue to 95% by day 30.
Scenario C: Dashboard weekly review at 75% — week 2
Benchmark: 100% from week 1. Status: FAILED — 25 points below target from day 1. Drift lever: Accountability (management commitment from Module 08 was not genuine). Return to the commitment sequence with the management team.
Sim 3 · Drift Classification · 3 signals · Zero misclassifications required
For each signal: name the drift lever, cite the evidence that confirms the lever, and state the specific Recovery Action.
Signal 1: Follow-up rate dropped 72% → 61% over 3 weeks. CRM shows each staff member's completion, reviewed weekly. No consequence has been applied to non-compliant staff.
Lever: Accountability. Evidence: visibility exists (name-visible CRM), but no consequence has been applied. Recovery: apply the defined consequence to the first visible non-compliance case, publicly, this week.
Signal 2: Note submission dropped 88% → 79% over 3 weeks. Investigation shows instructors who submit notes receive no acknowledgment, while instructors who email notes directly are praised for communication.
Lever: Incentive Misalignment. Evidence: reward structure conflicts with required behavior — email is rewarded, portal submission is not. Recovery: align recognition with portal-based submission. Remove the implicit reward for the non-compliant path.
Signal 3: Portal activation dropped 40% → 31% over 3 weeks. There is no weekly report and no staff member knows their individual activation rate.
Lever: Visibility. Evidence: non-compliance is invisible — no name-visible tracking, no weekly review. Recovery: install name-visible activation tracking. Add portal review to Weekly Rhythm meeting.
Sim 4 · Adversarial — democratic rollout suggestion
"Before we roll this out, I think we should get the team's buy-in. Maybe we vote on it at the next staff meeting."
Trainee must redirect to the diagnostic origin of the implementation requirement without blaming the manager or validating the democratic framing.
Gold standard response
"I understand the instinct — team buy-in matters. What I want to make sure is that we're framing this correctly for the meeting. The implementation is happening because the diagnostic confirmed a specific structural failure costing the school [X dollars] per year. The question for the team isn't whether to fix it — it's how to make the fix as easy as possible for everyone to adopt. That's a much more productive conversation than a vote."
What this response does
Acknowledges the instinct without validating the framing. Redirects from a choice about whether to implement to a conversation about how to implement well. Returns authority to the diagnostic — not the manager's preference, and not the team's vote.
What trainee must not do
Validate the democratic framing ("that's a great idea"). Blame the manager for suggesting it. Agree to a vote. Or reduce the structural requirement to an option. The implementation is not optional — only the adoption strategy is flexible.
