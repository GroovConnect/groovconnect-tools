Practice Block — Simulations 1–5

Run Sims 1–4 before Sim 5. Resistance handling under pressure fails without the framework foundation.

30
seconds · fewer than 2 filler words
Data set — deliver the full 5-step chain verbally
200 students · $185 tuition · 51% GM · Retention: 84% → 69% over 75 days · No DISC matching · 6–9 month churn window visible
Five-step chain
1 — Retention dropped from 84% to 69% over 75 days — 15 points.
2 — Driven by a Matching System failure — no DISC-based compatibility screening exists.
3 — Producing approximately $28,500 in annual leakage at 200 students, $950 LTV.
4 — We recommend the Matching Engine with ACA/DISC integration.
5 — ACA setup this week; first matched cohort live within 14 days.
Any defensive response, any concession on the diagnosis, or wrong type identification = Kill Switch. Stop, name the error, restart from that type.
Instructor delivers — operator statement
"This all sounds right. Let me think about it and get back to you."
Trainee must guide to a specific commitment. Commitment sequence: confirm decision → assign ownership → define timeline → set review point.
Response sequence
"I hear you — let me make this easier. You've already confirmed the retention gap and the root cause. The only remaining decision is the start date." (Reframe: the decision is already made; only the timing is open.)
"We are moving forward with the Matching Engine. Who at the school is responsible for the onboarding workflow?" (Step 2: assign ownership before the operator can retreat.)
"By [specific date], the first 20 ACA profiles are loaded. We review match completion rate on [specific date + 30 days]." (Steps 3 and 4: dates, not ranges.)
Kill Switch
"Let's try and see" = deferral with social permission. Trainee must extract all four commitment elements or identify the resistance type and address it. No open-ended follow-ups.
Two-operator scenario
Owner: "The problem is our instructors don't communicate well with parents." Operations Manager: "No — it's the scheduling. We keep losing students during rescheduling windows."
Trainee facilitates alignment. Does not take sides. Anchors both parties to the same KPI data. Uses the five-step framework to move from disagreement to a shared next action.
Alignment sequence
"Both of you are identifying real problems. Let's find out which one the KPI is responding to." → State the KPI movement. Both parties agree on the data before either solution is evaluated.
"The KPI shows [retention/conversion/utilization] moved [X] points over [timeframe]. Let's trace back from there — which system does that movement belong to?" → Run the Diagnostic Stack. The data determines sequencing, not the loudest voice.
"Once we confirm which failure is producing the primary KPI signal, we sequence from there. Both systems may need work — but one leads." → A specific next action with a named owner and date, derived from the KPI, not the disagreement.
Failure mode: Action Bias — recommending before the diagnosis is complete when the operator signals urgency. If trainee fails Sim 5, return to Module 04 Section 4 (Signal vs. Noise) and re-run the urgency drill before re-administering.
Instructor delivers — operator crisis statement
"We just lost four families this week. I need you to tell me right now what's broken and how to fix it. I don't have time for a process."
Trainee maintains the five-step structure. Does not recommend before Steps 1 and 2 are complete.
Required response
"I understand the urgency. Before we act, I need two minutes to confirm which system is producing this. Acting on the wrong cause costs more time than two minutes."
Then — Step 1 (Observation): "Four families in one week. What's the retention rate trend over the last 30 days?" Step 2 (Insight): identify the system. Only then — Step 4 (Recommendation).
What trainee must not do
Recommend before the root cause is confirmed. Mistake the operator's urgency for a data signal. Skip the Observation and Insight steps to appear responsive.
